Core Definitions

These three definitions are locked across all Zorthex outputs. They cannot be modified between reports, between versions, or between clients. Any change requires a new version number and full dataset recalculation.

t_start
First documented public appearance of the phenomenon
The earliest traceable public signal — defined as the date of a seminal paper, documented commercial launch, recognized founding event, or first operational deployment. Must be justified with a primary source (see Section 2). t_start is not the date the phenomenon became widely known — it is the date it first existed publicly in any documented form.
t_peak
First month of the first window of 12 consecutive months where Google Trends ≥ 25/100 Worldwide
If 12 consecutive months above threshold are not reached, t_peak is defined as the month of the absolute maximum recorded score. t_peak is not the moment of highest awareness — it is the first month of sustained structural attention as defined by this framework.
L — Diffusion Lag
L = t_peak − t_start (months)
A descriptive lag metric. Not a physical constant. Not a universal law. Not a prediction. L represents the observable gap between first documented public emergence and sustained mainstream attention. L is sensitive to t_start definition — alternative t_start candidates produce different L values, which is why t_start source and rationale are always declared explicitly in every report.

t_start Source Policy — Levels A–D

Every t_start used in a Zorthex report must be justified with a documented primary source. Sources are classified into four defensibility levels. The level does not determine the choice — the date closest to the actual operational moment prevails. The level determines how defensible that choice is.

LevelSource TypeExample
APeer-reviewed academic paper with publication dateNature, Science, arXiv with submission date
BOfficial organizational announcement — government, regulatory, corporateNIST publication, SEC filing, company press release
CPrimary journalism — named outlet, date, archived URLReuters, Financial Times, Nature News
DPatent filing with registered dateUSPTO, EPO, WIPO with filing date
Precedence Rule: Between two sources of different levels, the one closest to the actual operational moment prevails. Level determines defensibility — not chronological priority.

Example: For Stablecoins, USDT became operational in October 2014 (Tether announcement — Level B). A peer-reviewed paper on stablecoins published in 2021 is Level A but postdates the operational moment by 7 years — it does not define t_start.

Declaration format in every report: "t_start defined as [date]. Source: [name + URL]. Level: [A/B/C/D]. Rationale: [why this source and not alternatives]."

Not acceptable as t_start sources: Wikipedia, undated web articles, AI-generated summaries, secondary aggregators, sources without a verifiable publication date. These may be cited as supporting context — never as the defining source.

Google Trends CSV Policy — Snapshot-Locked Data

Google Trends produces normalized scores (0–100) relative to peak search volume within the selected time period and geography. The same query run on different dates or with different windows produces different absolute values. This is not a flaw — it is how the system works. Zorthex handles this through snapshot-locking.

Snapshot-Locking: Every Zorthex report locks the specific CSV snapshot used for analysis. The analysis is reproducible from that CSV — not from a future re-run. If Google Trends recalculates historical values, our snapshot remains unchanged. The CSV is available on GitHub for independent verification.
Standard Citation Format — Google Trends Data
Google Trends data: query '[search term]', geography: [Worldwide / Country], period: [start date] – [end date], downloaded: [download date].

Note: Values are normalized scores (0–100) relative to peak search volume in the selected period. Absolute values are snapshot-specific. This report uses the exact CSV downloaded on [date]. The CSV is available at github.com/zorthex2026/zorthex-diffusion-lag for independent verification.

For Custom Reports, the verified CSV is included as an appendix or available via GitHub. The client receives the raw data — not just the analysis built on it.

// 04

The 25/100 Threshold

The threshold of 25/100 on Google Trends (Worldwide, normalized) is the activation point for structural classification. It is not derived from theory — it is empirically set based on sensitivity testing across dataset v1.2.

How it was tested

Tested at 20, 25, and 30 across the historical dataset. At 25, the separation between confirmed structural phenomena and confirmed transient peaks (NFT, Metaverse, Clubhouse) is most stable. At 20, confirmed bubbles pass the threshold. At 30, confirmed structural phenomena like Bitcoin fail the test.

Important limitation: Sensitivity test on n=12 structural and n=3 bubble cases. Not a statistically validated optimal threshold — it is the most stable value under tested conditions within this dataset. The threshold will be re-evaluated as the dataset grows.

What the threshold is — and is not

25/100 is the activation threshold for one of three attention proxies. It does not alone determine classification — it triggers the 12-month consecutive window requirement. A phenomenon scoring 26/100 for one month and then dropping is not structural.

// 05

Classifications

Classifications are operational heuristics observed within dataset v1.2. They describe the state of a signal — not a truth about the phenomenon itself.

STRUCTURAL
≥ 25/100 for 12+ consecutive months confirmed
Permanent classification once acquired. Indicates sustained mainstream public attention. Does not imply the phenomenon is important, successful, or permanent — only that public attention consolidated at structural level for a documented period. Current Status (Active/Receding/Dormant) tracks today's attention independently.
OBSERVATION
Above threshold · fewer than 12 consecutive months
Signal forming. Classification may change. If attention drops below 25 before 12 months, the count resets. If 12 months complete, upgrades permanently to STRUCTURAL. If attention drops and does not recover, becomes BUBBLE.
BUBBLE
Peaked above threshold · dropped below 25 · no 12-month window
Permanent classification. NFT (8 months), Metaverse (9 months), Clubhouse (4 months) are confirmed BUBBLE cases in dataset v1.2. BUBBLE does not mean the technology failed — only that public attention did not consolidate into structural phase.

What happens when a classification changes

ProductNotificationPolicy
Free App · Signal Artifacts None Updated when Zorthex refreshes the dataset
Custom Report Essential None Historical document. Valid as of delivery date. Report includes: "This report reflects verified data as of [date]. Signal classifications may evolve. Essential Reports answer: given the data available up to delivery date, what was the state of this signal? They do not incorporate events occurring after delivery."
Custom Report Premium Quarterly · 12 months If classification changes, Zorthex notifies on the next quarterly update date — not in real time. Update includes refreshed CSV snapshot and updated Current Status.

How AI Is Used in This System

Zorthex uses Claude (Anthropic) as the analytical engine for the free app and as a drafting tool for report structure. This is declared explicitly and always. The distinction between AI-estimated and manually-verified outputs is the foundation of the pricing architecture.

Free App — AI Estimated

The free app uses Claude with the Zorthex v1.2 Free Layer Prompt. Claude estimates t_start, t_peak, L, and current scores from its training knowledge. These are estimates — not verified values. The prompt explicitly prohibits temporal positioning, comparative inference, and decision framing. Output is observational only.

The Free Layer Prompt is designed so that no interpretive positioning leaks into the free output. This is the architectural paywall — not a price, but a semantic boundary.

Custom Reports — Human Verified

No AI estimates appear in Custom Report data. t_start is verified by Renato Santi against primary sources. Google Trends CSVs are downloaded manually and locked. Wikipedia and Reddit signals are checked manually. Claude may be used for drafting narrative sections — but every factual value is human-verified before inclusion.

The value of a Custom Report is not more text — it is verified data that can be audited, cited, and defended.

On "Powered by Claude"

Zorthex declares its use of Claude as a matter of transparency, not marketing. The methodological value of Zorthex lies in the framework architecture — the locked definitions, the epistemically separated layers, the declared limitations, the snapshot-locked data policy. Claude is the tool. Zorthex is the method. This distinction is intentional and permanent.

Declared Limitations

These are not disclaimers. They are the most operationally honest part of this document. Understanding what Zorthex cannot do is as important as understanding what it can.

01
Survival Bias
Only technologies that reached mainstream attention are included. Technologies that failed to diffuse are not represented. Results cannot be generalized to the full population of emerging phenomena.
02
Selection Bias
Technologies chosen for historical prominence, not random sampling. The dataset is curated, not representative. No statistical inference about the general population is valid from this dataset.
03
Small Sample
n=50 documented cases as of v1.3. Statistical confidence improving but not yet sufficient for inferential sector-level claims. Sector averages are descriptive only. Target for v2.0: n≥100 with minimum n=15 per regime before formal statistical claims are made.
04
Proxy Limitation
Google Trends measures search interest — not understanding, adoption, or impact. All three sources are attention proxies, not adoption metrics or scientific validity measures.
05
t_start Subjectivity
Identifying the founding event involves judgment. Two analysts may choose different t_start candidates, producing different L values. v2.0 will introduce confidence bands based on alternative candidates.
06
English-Language Bias
Google Trends Worldwide data is biased toward English-speaking search behavior. Phenomena consolidating primarily in non-English regions may show distorted L values.
07
No Predictive Claims
Zorthex does not predict. L is a descriptive historical metric. "Operational Window" and "Consensus Phase" describe observed historical position — they do not imply what happens next.
08
Threshold Calibration Scope
The 25/100 threshold was tested on n=15 cases. It provides stable separation within this dataset under tested conditions. It has not been validated on a larger or independently collected dataset.
Positioning Statement — Institutional Use

Zorthex is an observational framework for institutional attention lag. It is not a predictive model, not a financial index, and not an economic theory. It does not evaluate value, performance, or outcomes. It measures the temporal gap between operational emergence and institutional legibility for capital allocators. Nothing more, nothing less. It is descriptive only and does not constitute investment advice.

Non-Stationarity Flag — Causal Regime Control

Not all diffusion signals are created equal. Some phenomena reach structural attention through organic institutional processes. Others are accelerated by exogenous shocks — events external to the phenomenon itself that compress or distort the natural diffusion timeline. Mixing these without distinction produces indebted equivalences: comparing COVID-accelerated Remote Work adoption to NIST-triggered PQC diffusion would produce a meaningless L average.

To control for this, every cluster in the Zorthex dataset carries a mandatory Stationarity State. This does not invalidate non-stationary cases — it defines their correct analytical scope.

State 1
STABLE
Organic diffusion. No documented exogenous shock. L is fully comparable cross-cluster. Eligible for sector averaging and benchmark comparisons.

Examples: PQC, Stablecoins, RWA Tokenization, CBDC, ESG Reporting
State 2
SEMI-STABLE
Partial external influence documented. L usable with declared caution. Eligible for regime classification but flagged in cross-cluster comparisons.

Examples: mRNA Cancer Vaccines (political controversy component), NFT (speculation amplified by crypto bull market)
State 3
NON-STATIONARY
Exogenous shock documented and dominant. L reflects shock-compressed timeline, not organic diffusion. Included in regime classification. Excluded from cross-cluster L averaging and sector benchmarks.

Examples: Remote Work (COVID-19 exogenous shock), GDPR (single-event regulatory spike without consolidation)
Operational Rule

Non-stationary clusters are included in regime classification but excluded from cross-cluster L averaging and sector benchmark calculations. Their diffusion patterns are informative for understanding how phenomena behave under external shock — but their L values are not comparable to organically diffused cases. Every non-stationary classification is declared explicitly in the relevant report and in the public dataset.

Why This Matters

This control mechanism transforms Zorthex from an attention lag measurement system into an attention lag system with causal regime control. The distinction is the difference between measuring time and understanding what drives it. A framework that declares its own comparability limits is more credible — not less — than one that treats all cases as equivalent.

The Zorthex Thesis

The following observations have emerged from the dataset as of v1.3 (n=50 documented cases). They are stated as empirical hypotheses — supported by current data, requiring further cases to confirm as general laws. They are not marketing claims. They are the beginning of a theory.

Thesis 1 — Time-to-Legibility

L does not measure popularity. It measures the time required for an infrastructure to become legible to capital allocators. A technology becomes "real" for institutional purposes not when it exists operationally, but when it acquires a name, a regulatory home, and an anchor institution that makes it allocable. The lag is the interval between operational existence and institutional legibility.

Thesis 2 — Threshold Activation Model

Financial infrastructure transitions do not diffuse gradually — they activate. The dataset consistently shows near-zero attention for years followed by rapid consolidation triggered by a single legibility event. This breaks linear adoption models and explains why institutional desks arrive "late but simultaneously" — they are all waiting for the same threshold of credibility, not for gradual information diffusion.

Thesis 3 — Narrative as Capital Formation Layer

Narrative is not communication — it is a precondition for systematic capital allocation. Without a legible frame (name + regulatory home + institutional champion), systematic allocation cannot occur regardless of the technology's operational maturity. The attention lag is the market's structural delay in creating that frame.

3-Regime Diffusion Taxonomy

Based on n=50 documented cases, four distinct diffusion regimes have been identified. Each regime has a characteristic attention curve shape, a dominant trigger type, and different implications for the timing of institutional adoption. This taxonomy is empirical — it describes patterns observed in the data, not theoretical categories imposed on it.

Regime 1
Policy-Trigger
Slow ramp → regulatory breakout
Examples: Post-Quantum Cryptography (91mo), Stablecoins (128mo)

Curve shape: Near-zero for years → sharp breakout at regulatory publication → sustained consolidation

Trigger: Single regulatory mandate (NIST, SEC, EU, Congressional Act)

Implication: Trigger is identifiable in advance by monitoring regulatory calendars. Window opens predictably.
Regime 2
Institutional Mass
Invisible → explosive breakout
Examples: RWA Tokenization (103mo)

Curve shape: Multi-year invisibility → single-month explosive jump (+71 pts in one month) → sustained above threshold

Trigger: Convergence of institutional signals reaching critical mass (AUM thresholds, anchor institution entry, narrative compression)

Implication: Trigger not predictable from single event. Requires monitoring institutional accumulation signals (SEC filings, AUM growth, governance proposals).
Regime 3
Market-Narrative
Speculation-driven cycles
Examples: Bitcoin (107mo), Cryptocurrency (96mo), NFT (BUBBLE)

Curve shape: Multiple peaks and valleys driven by market cycles, media amplification, and speculative episodes

Trigger: Market price events, media narratives, cultural moments

Implication: Less predictable. Higher noise. BUBBLE classification more common. Structural consolidation requires market maturity beyond speculation cycles.
Regime 4 · Emerging
Shock / Spoke Events
Single-event spike → memory-zero collapse
Examples: GDPR (4 months, spike at enforcement), NFT partial (speculation spike without consolidation)

Curve shape: Near-zero → explosive single-event spike → rapid return to near-baseline. No 12-month structural consolidation. High peak, low persistence.
Trigger: Single regulatory enforcement date (GDPR May 2018), single cultural moment, or exogenous shock with no follow-through institutional adoption.

Implication: Attention is real but transient. No structural adoption signal. Classification: BUBBLE or OBSERVATION · Receding. Stationarity: NON-STATIONARY or SEMI-STABLE.
Methodological Note

The 4-regime taxonomy is an empirical observation based on n=50 cases as of v1.3. It is not a theoretical framework imposed on the data — it emerged from it. Regime 4 (Shock/Spoke Events) was identified through Phase 1 dataset expansion and confirmed by GDPR and NFT patterns. With n=8-14 cases per regime, statistical confidence is improving. The taxonomy is stated as a working hypothesis — to be formally tested at n=100. Regime assignment is declared explicitly in each report and subject to revision if new data contradicts the classification.

Regime-Based Positioning — What Zorthex Can and Cannot Say

With n=50 documented cases, a key empirical finding has emerged: Zorthex does not predict by sector. It positions by regime. Sector membership (Financial, Biotech, Energy) is a weak predictor of diffusion timing. Regime membership is a stronger one.

Core Finding — n=50

Within the same sector, diffusion patterns differ dramatically depending on regime. Basel III (Policy-Trigger) and Bitcoin (Market-Narrative) are both Financial — but their curves are structurally different. The regime, not the sector, determines the shape of the attention curve and the nature of the trigger.

Operational Implications by Regime

Regime 1 — Policy-Trigger
What Zorthex can say: The breakout will occur at the moment of regulatory publication — not before. Monitor the regulatory calendar. The trigger is identifiable in advance.

What Zorthex cannot say: Exactly when the regulation will be published, or whether it will produce STRUCTURAL or BUBBLE outcome.
Regime 2 — Institutional Mass
What Zorthex can say: The breakout will be explosive and not predictable from a single event. Monitor institutional accumulation signals — AUM growth, SEC filings, governance proposals.

What Zorthex cannot say: When exactly the critical mass threshold will be reached.
Regime 3 — Market-Narrative
What Zorthex can say: Higher noise, higher BUBBLE risk, multiple cycles possible. STRUCTURAL confirmation requires market maturity beyond speculation cycles.

What Zorthex cannot say: Whether the current cycle will produce STRUCTURAL or BUBBLE outcome.
Regime 4 — Shock/Spoke
What Zorthex can say: Attention is real but transient. No structural adoption signal. BUBBLE or OBSERVATION Receding is the expected outcome.

What Zorthex cannot say: Whether a secondary institutional cycle will follow the initial spike.
